Problem-Solving Reflections


Blocked on a Simple Problem

  • Problem: I've been trying to implement a case-insensitive search in an array of strings, and despite it seeming like a straightforward task, I just couldn’t seem to get it right no matter how much I tried. It felt like such a simple problem, but I found myself stuck.
  • Techniques: I started by reading through the code a lot, trying to break down and simplify the issue. To help understand the problem, I added some console.log statements throughout the code. After that, I decided to Google the problem, looking for examples and potential solutions for similar situations.
  • Feelings: Initially, I felt frustrated with myself for being unable to solve what seemed like a basic problem. But as I began to break it down step by step, I felt my focus returning, and eventually, I experienced a sense of relief once I successfully resolved the issue.
  • Lesson: This experience taught me that even tasks that seem simple require attention to detail and that I need to use all available tools, such as debugging techniques and online resources, to work through issues effectively.

Elegantly Solved a Problem

  • Problem: I needed to search for a specific object within an array and retrieve a particular property based on a specific condition.
  • Techniques: I employed the `.find()` method, which provided a concise, clean, and easily maintainable solution. It helped simplify the logic while ensuring efficiency.
  • Feelings: The elegance of this solution gave me a strong sense of confidence and satisfaction. I felt reassured that sometimes the simple approaches can be the most effective, often resulting in both simplicity and efficiency in the code.
  • Lesson: This experience confirmed for me that striving to achieve the best approach frequently leads to simplicity and efficiency.

Building Confidence in Problem-Solving Techniques

  • Pseudocode: I feel moderately confident using pseudocode — it helps me organize my thoughts and structure the problem. In saying this, sometimes I skip this step when I’m too excited to dive into coding.
  • Trying something: I'm very confident in trying different approaches and experimenting. I believe it’s a critical part of programming, and testing out different ideas often leads to effective solutions.
  • Rubber ducky method: I feel fairly confident in using the rubber ducky method — explaining problems out loud often helps me reach conclusions that I wouldn’t have reached otherwise.
  • Read error messages: I feel very sure about this — reading error messages is always my first step in troubleshooting, as they offer clear clues to identify what's going wrong.
  • Console.logging: I'm very confident in using console.log statements — I feel like it's crucial for understanding how my code is working and pinpointing where issues are.
  • Googling: I’m very confident in Googling — it's an essential tool for researching and finding solutions to similar problems or for learning about new techniques.
  • Asking peers for help: I feel moderately confident — I know it’s valuable to ask for input, but I sometimes hesitate because I don’t want to appear less competent and I'm quite shy haha.
  • Asking coaches: I'm moderately confident — I typically ask for help when I’m really stuck, but I know that I could reach out to someone sooner rather than waiting.
  • Reflecting: I feel quite confident in reflecting — I understand the importance of reflection, but I know that I could make it a more regular part of my process.

Reluctant to Ask for Help

  • Situation: I’ve often hesitated to ask for help when facing tricky logic, mainly because I worry it might make me seem less competent to others.
  • Next Time: Moving forward, I’ll remind myself that asking for help is perfectly normal and that it’s okay to reach out sooner rather than later. I’ll embrace the fact that seeking guidance is a sign of strength, not weakness, and I’ll make an effort to view it as an essential part of problem-solving.
